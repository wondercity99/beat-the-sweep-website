Sweep FAQs

Everything you wanted to know about street sweeping but were too busy moving your car to ask.

When did street sweeping start and who started it?

The practice of keeping city streets clean goes back centuries, but for most of that history it was the job of individual property owners to clean the road in front of their address, as required by local ordinance. The first organized public street sweeping program in America is credited to Benjamin Franklin, who set it up in Philadelphia using paid crews with brooms — the work back then was mostly mud and horse manure.

Mechanical sweeping arrived in 1843, when Joseph Whitworth invented a horse-drawn sweeping machine in Manchester, England, which was then considered one of the unhealthiest cities in the world thanks to its massive textile industry. The first U.S. patent came in 1849 from C.S. Bishop, also horse-drawn. Motorized street sweeping started in 1913, when John M. Murphy’s Elgin Sweeper Company sold the first power sweeper to Boise, Idaho. The basic technology stayed largely unchanged until the 1970s, when environmental concerns fundamentally reshaped the job.

Why do cities sweep the streets?

Three reasons, roughly in order of importance. First, water quality: most storm drains lead directly to rivers, lakes, or the ocean, so anything that accumulates on the street — sediment, leaves, oil, tire rubber, brake dust, heavy metals — ends up there after the next rain. Since the Clean Water Act of 1972, the EPA has considered regular street sweeping a best management practice for controlling stormwater pollution.

Second, air quality: vehicles kick up fine particulate matter from road surfaces, particularly particles under 10 microns (PM10) and 2.5 microns (PM2.5) that reach deep into the lungs, and sweeping is one of the only tools cities have for reducing this non-exhaust pollution at the source.

Third, drainage and safety: clogged storm drains cause flooding, wet leaves create slip hazards, and in cold climates blocked drains mean water refreezes on roads overnight. Aesthetics and litter removal matter too, but they’re secondary to the environmental story.

Does it actually do anything to clean the streets?

Yes — but it depends heavily on the equipment. Modern regenerative-air and vacuum sweepers, which are certified to collect particles down to 2.5 microns, genuinely work. A 2024 Toronto study found a regenerative-air sweeper removed 76% of thoracic-sized (<10μm) road dust on average, and well-run programs can cut stormwater pollutant loads by up to 80%.

The catch is that roughly 90% of the street sweepers currently in service in the U.S. are the older mechanical broom type. These are much better at moving visible debris than capturing fine particles, and they can genuinely push some material around rather than collecting it. Recent controlled studies have also found that when old-style sweeping does reduce pollution levels, the effect is often modest or not statistically significant, and air quality improvements tend to be short-term.

So: if your city runs a modern vacuum sweeper, it’s doing real work. If they’re still running 1980s-era mechanical brooms, some of the skepticism is earned.

Is street sweeping just a money grab by cities?

It’s easy to see why people suspect this. Street sweeping tickets are among the most common parking citations issued in major U.S. cities — in Los Angeles they accounted for over 26% of all parking citations in the first half of 2024, totaling an estimated $17.6 million in revenue. Chicago pulls in more than $15 million a year from sweeping tickets alone. That’s a lot of money attached to a service most residents never directly see.

But the evidence mostly points to “regulatory tool with revenue attached” rather than “pure revenue scheme.” Ticket revenue typically flows into a city’s general fund rather than to the department that actually runs the sweepers, so the people doing the work don’t get a kickback. The programs themselves are expensive to operate — Chicago’s costs about $8.5 million a year, and a single modern sweeper truck runs around $168,000 before maintenance. And fundamentally, cities can’t sweep a street that’s full of parked cars, so the ticket exists to enforce clearance, not to generate revenue on sweeping day.

That said, there are legitimate abuse cases. Philadelphia — the city that invented American street sweeping — hasn’t had a regular citywide program since the early 2000s, but cars can still be ticketed under the rules. Confusing signage, last-minute postings, and fines that can run $70 or more for an honest mistake create real resentment even in cities where the program is run in good faith.

The honest answer is that it’s a legitimate public service whose enforcement model generates significant revenue, and whose punitive edge falls hardest on people who couldn’t afford to lose the money and didn’t see the sign in time. Both things are true at once.
